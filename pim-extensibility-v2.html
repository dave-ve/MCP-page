The old way of thinking about extensions

Extensions used to mean one thing: a clickable feature added to your PIM interface. Something your team could open, configure, and run to cover a specific need — connect a system, trigger a workflow, surface a capability. Built for humans. Built for predefined, rigid actions. Useful, but limited to what a person decided to click.

That mental model shaped how the whole industry talked about extensibility. Add a connector. Add a feature. Add a button. The question was always: what else can we put in the UI?

AI agents need tools to operate — not just data to read.

That's the shift that changes everything. The question is no longer "can AI read my PIM data?" — every vendor can claim that.

The real question

Can I add capabilities to my PIM that AI can operate — the way a human does?

On most platforms

The answer is no.

Extensions are clickable features in the UI, built for a person to open and run. Whatever the vendor pre-built is what you get — no path to build your own that fit the way your business actually works, and no way for an AI agent to operate them with the same precision your team has.

On Bluestone PIM

The answer is yes — and you can extend it.

Native Bluestone capabilities are operable through UI, API, and MCP with full parity. And when you need more, you can build your own custom extensions natively inside the platform — and those become agent-operable tools too.

Already extensive. Always extensible.

And on top of all that — extensions you build yourself

Here's where the shift becomes concrete. When your operation has a workflow no one else has — and almost every enterprise has some — your team builds it. Not as a separate app bolted to the PIM through an API, but as a native extension that runs inside Bluestone PIM, on our SDK, using our UI component library, registered into the same SaaS environment everything else lives in.

How can you use extensions?

This is what 700+ granular API endpoints, full UI/API/MCP parity, and a native SDK make possible. Every native capability on Bluestone PIM — and every custom extension your team builds — is reachable through three consumption channels: UI, API, and MCP. Use whichever fit your operation today. The others are there when you want them — without rebuilding anything.

For your team

Through the UI

The way you'd expect: panels, dashboards, inline editors, workflows. Whether the capability is one Bluestone built or one your team built, it looks and behaves like part of the platform. One interface to train your team on. One UX vocabulary. One place to work.

For AI agents

Through the MCP server

Bluestone PIM ships with a Model Context Protocol server. Any agent that speaks the protocol — Claude, ChatGPT, internal copilots — can call native platform capabilities directly as tools. And because of full UI/API/MCP parity on the same runtime, the custom extension your team built for the UI is automatically available to agents too.

Build a tool once. Your team runs it in the UI. Your agents run it through MCP. Same code, same permissions, same audit trail.
For your systems

Through 700+ APIs

The API surface that's always been there. Documented, granular, OAuth2/OpenID Connect authenticated. Your ERP, your e-commerce platforms, your marketplaces, your data warehouses — they integrate against the same capabilities your team and your agents are using.

Every vendor will eventually ship an MCP server for what they built. Only a platform with 700+ granular API endpoints, full UI/API/MCP parity, and a native SDK lets you build the tools your agents need for what they didn't. That's what Bluestone PIM is.

The compounding economics

Once you operate this way, the rest of the maths follows.

01 · Better fit

Pay for what fits. Build what doesn't.

You stop paying for features you don't use, and you stop tolerating gaps where a SaaS doesn't quite match your business.

  • Native capabilities cover the common ground
  • Custom extensions cover what's unique to you
  • Nothing in between
02 · Fewer vendors

One platform, less procurement.

When your build path is fast and the result lives inside one platform, the case for procuring a separate tool for every adjacent capability gets weaker.

  • Fewer contracts and renewals
  • Fewer security reviews
  • Fewer integrations to maintain
  • Fewer training programmes

The operational simplification matters as much as the savings.

03 · The compounding one

Compounding AI leverage.

Every extension your team builds is also a new tool your AI agents can use. The platform gets more powerful for AI workflows in proportion to how much you've built on it. Teams that adopt this model early will have an operational gap with teams that didn't — and the gap will widen.

Your team gets a UI tool
Your agents get an MCP tool
Your systems get an API
04 · Faster iteration

You change with your business.

A capability your team built can be modified by your team. A SaaS product you bought has a roadmap controlled by someone else. When your business changes — and it will — you change with it.

05 · Durable architecture

Composable. Modular. Replaceable.

Composable means modular. Modular means replaceable. The MACH Alliance certification isn't a sticker — it's a commitment to the principle that no part of your stack should hold the rest of it hostage. You're not locked in because the architecture doesn't lock anyone in.
